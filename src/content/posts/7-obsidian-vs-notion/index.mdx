## Context

I've been using Notion for about 5 years. I started with lecture notes at university and lately have been maintaining notes related to my work. Not to mention the shared notes with my wife to plan vacations, write down yummy recipes, and make a list of movies to watch.

Notion is a great tool, and I don't have any critical reason why I decided to switch over to Obsidian. I'd say the main reason is just my curiosity and willingness to try something new. So let's list the things I liked most about using Obsidian.

## Storage and Files Format

The first thing is related to Obsidian's design. By default, Obsidian doesn't keep your notes on its servers; everything is stored on your machine. The file format of notes is just `.md` files, which means it's pretty universal and you can use it wherever you want. For example, I wrote this post in Obsidian and published it on this blog, as my blog supports `.md` files.

Of course, this comes at a cost: you won't be able to open your notes saved on a laptop from an iPhone. For that, you probably want to store the files on iCloud. Or there's another option: you can create an Obsidian account and pay for Obsidian Sync ($4 per month). But this, besides being yet another subscription burden, gives your data to Obsidian. They have encryption, and I don't think this is a major risk, but it's a totally different vibe.

Also, because Obsidian doesn't store your files by default, it can be used for notes at work. For example, I know there are companies that prohibit the use of Notion because of data policies, as it does store your notes on its servers, which raises data security issues. I can't think of a reason why a company would ban Obsidian, as it's just a text editor with some cool features. And if you don't pay for the Sync feature, the files are yours to keep.

Finally, as I didn't use Notion on my iPhone, I don't plan to use Obsidian either, so I have no problem with storing everything locally on my laptop.

## Text editing

It's Markdown, I mean, is there anything to add here? Well, maybe the fact that if you are one of those pianists and like Vim, it has you covered, as you can switch on Vim motions and do things like `hjklweb0$i`, etc. If you know, you know.

Personally, I am not a skilled Vim operator and quite like my mouse. However, just for the sake of my experiment, I decided to try using Vim motions. I think that in terms of post editing, the use case is quite limited, and I don't need to remember all the possible commands. Navigating within the post using only the keyboard is nice. Anyway, it's good to have this feature.

Other than that, by default the text editor was in preview mode, so I switched it to plain Markdown so that I can see all the special characters.

## Customization

In comparison to Notion, the default functionality of Obsidian is not that rich, but there are endless opportunities, as you can find plugins from community members or from Obsidian itself.

Every time I had a thought like "It would've been nice to have feature X," I was able to find a plugin created by some nice community member that solved my issue. Needless to say, if there's no such plugin, you can develop it yourself. Especially given the availability of AI agents, just vibe-code your little plugin to solve your niche pain. Maybe your pain is not that unique after all, and other people might even say "Thank you!" in the future.

And don't forget that you can develop your own plugins. With the help of AI, it's really just a matter of communicating your wants to a chatbot.

## Using AI

Speaking of AI! I am paying for ChatGPT, and I use it quite a lot, but it isn't cheap. As my main app for notes was Notion, when I wanted to do something there with AI, I had to manually copy everything and ask ChatGPT in its web application.

Another option could have been to start paying for Notion AI, and it works great, I had a trial period and loved it. But this is another $20 per month! And this doesn't substitute ChatGPT, as OpenAI also provides Codex, which I use as an agent to help with coding.

Now the greatest part: you can install Codex in the terminal (Codex CLI), and then launch it with the context of whatever folder you have. Codex will be able to read everything, but most importantly, create and edit files.

So, as everything I write with Obsidian is basically `.md` files, I have Codex CLI that accesses those files and does whatever I ask it to: fix grammar, generate ideas, do research, and document the results. This is just a tiny list of what I actually do with it, but I hope you get the idea. And I don't have to pay for another AI solution, everything is included in my ChatGPT subscription.

## Conclusion

So far so good. This is by no means a deep dive into Obsidian functionality. I would also mention that high customizability is a double-edged sword. It's true that you can find a lot of useful plugins to solve pretty much any pain you have, but in Notion, from the get-go, many more features are available, so you don't even need to think about plugins.

Let this post be more of a first impression after roughly a couple of weeks of using it. I haven't had a chance to test it at work, as I start on April 15th. What I can tell is that I want to start making those work-related notes in Obsidian, and we will see how this goes.

I hope to keep you updated on this matter, but for now I want to say thank you so much for reading! I wish you a great day.
